Module 01 — Forex Fundamentals

Currency Pairs Explained

⏱ 8 min read 📊 Beginner ✓ Lesson 3 of 5

What is a Currency Pair?

In forex trading, currencies are always quoted in pairs. When you trade EUR/USD, you're simultaneously buying the Euro and selling the US Dollar — or vice versa. The price of a currency pair tells you how much of the quote currency (the second one) you need to buy one unit of the base currency (the first one).

For example, if EUR/USD = 1.1532, it means 1 Euro buys 1.1532 US Dollars. If you believe the Euro will strengthen against the Dollar, you buy EUR/USD. If you think it will weaken, you sell it.

💡 Key Insight

You never trade a single currency in isolation. Every forex trade involves two currencies, which is why understanding the relative strength between two economies is the foundation of fundamental analysis.

Major, Minor and Exotic Pairs

Forex pairs are divided into three main categories based on their liquidity and the currencies involved:

  • Major Pairs — Always include the USD. Examples: EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, AUD/USD. Most liquid, lowest spreads.
  • Minor Pairs (Crosses) — Don't include USD. Examples: EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY. Slightly wider spreads.
  • Exotic Pairs — One major currency + one from an emerging economy. Examples: USD/TRY, EUR/ZAR. Wide spreads, high volatility.

For fundamental traders, major pairs are the most practical to analyze because there is more macroeconomic data, central bank coverage, and news flow available.

Base Currency
The first currency in a pair. In EUR/USD, EUR is the base. You're measuring how many USD you need to buy 1 EUR.
Quote Currency
The second currency in a pair. In EUR/USD, USD is the quote currency. The price reflects its value in USD.
Bid Price
The price at which the market (broker) will buy the base currency from you. Always slightly lower than ask.
Ask Price
The price at which the market will sell the base currency to you. Always slightly higher than bid.
Spread
The difference between bid and ask. This is the broker's fee. Tighter spreads = lower cost of trading.
Pip
The smallest standard price move. For most pairs = 0.0001. For JPY pairs = 0.01. Used to measure profit/loss.

Why Currency Pairs Matter for Fundamentals

Understanding pairs is crucial for fundamental analysis because every pair represents a comparison between two economies. When you analyze EUR/USD, you're not just looking at Europe or the US in isolation — you're comparing their relative economic health, interest rates, inflation, and growth prospects.

This is why two currencies with very different central bank policies (one hiking, one cutting) often see large directional moves. The pair's price reflects the market's view of that divergence.
